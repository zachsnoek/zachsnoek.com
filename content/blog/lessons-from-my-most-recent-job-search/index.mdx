I joined the [Fabric](https://meetfabric.com/careers) team last August after a two-month, full-time job search. This was the first time that I've had to do a "real" search process; I found my previous two roles pretty quickly, largely due to luck.

This process was a _huge_ learning experience, so I'm writing down my biggest takeaways for my future self and anyone else who finds this helpful. I've loosely categorized items, and I may come back to update this list as I remember things.

## Finances

### Build an emergency fund before you need it

Calculate your runway and get an emergency fund in place so that you aren't making career decisions out of desperation or urgency. I wrote more about this [here](/blog/tips-and-lessons-learned-from-my-sabbatical).

### Know your worth

When recruiters would ask me my desired salary, I would usually say something like, "I'm pretty flexible. Ideally, I'd like $x–y, but I'd be willing to take as low as $z."

"$z" was actually 60% less than the offer I ultimately accepted. The point is: do your market research and don't undervalue yourself. The worst they can say to your ask is no 🤷‍♀️.

However, you also likely can't wait forever for your perfect salary, and you should know when your financial reality requires you to lower your asking price. Consider mapping your salary floor to a timeline, for example:

-   Now until Mar 1: $50k min
-   Starting Mar 15: $45k min
-   Starting Apr 1: $40k min

### Financial stability improves performance

Knowing that you can fund your current expenses can relieve the pressure to immediately land a job, which may improve your interview performance. More generally, aim to reduce distractions and improve concentration during interviews.

## Recruiters

### Vet your recruiters

Avoid recruiters that ghost you and cut ties as soon as you receive a rejection. Good recruiters should want to build a long-term relationship.

Prioritize those who provide detailed prep material and gather interviewer feedback.

### Value communicators

Work with recruiters that keep you updated even when there is no news and take the time to offer genuine career advice and feedback. Multiple recruiters were kind enough to offer their advice on my job search and offers I received, and their feedback led to a great decision.

## Interviews

### Prepare for a slow start

I found that it typically takes at least two weeks to move from a recruiter screen to a first-round interview. If you have prep materials for the first round, schedule time on your calendar to prepare!

### Set boundaries

Aim for companies with four rounds or fewer, and don't let the process drag on too long. Personally, I don't want to spend more than a month interviewing with one company.

Avoid impersonal AI, recorded, or asynchronous interviews. A good company should value your time and care about meeting candidates.

### Schedule interviews realistically

Don't overbook yourself and know how many interviews you can handle each day and week. I could do 1–2 recruiter calls per day, and I tried not to do more than two major interviews (e.g., behavioral, technical) each week.

You may just have to roll with the punches, though: I had 11(!) interviews during my final two weeks.

Also, don't schedule interviews too early in the day. Be realistic with the minimum amount of time it takes to get ready for an interview and be awake enough to perform well. After a sleepier-than-usual 9 a.m. interview, I created a rule for no interviews before 10 a.m.

I found that 10–11:30 a.m. was my interviewing sweet spot: I was not too tired and not too hungry. I did, however, do some interviews later in the day around 4–5 p.m. that went well.

### Have an interview day routine

Set up your space consistently before interviews and make sure you have everything you need. My process had a few steps like these:

-   Ensure my AirPods are charged
-   Run through my elevator pitch
-   Fill up a glass of water for my desk
-   Make sure my lip balm, pen, and paper are in reach
-   Set the lighting in my office to "video call" mode
-   Open the video call, my question list, etc. on my computer

### Accept every interview for practice

Mock interviews are great, but you won't really know how you perform until you do real interviews. When you're starting out, take any interview that comes your way for real-world practice. Go in to these interviews expecting to fail. Gather feedback, improve, and keep trying.

### You can't predict the outcome

Before this job search, I always heard colleagues and other engineers say that they could fail interviews they thought went great and pass interviews they thought they bombed.

This is 100% true: I failed an interview I thought I passed with flying colors, and passed an interview I felt iffy about.

## Timing and wellbeing

### Consider taking a sabbatical before you start

I took intentional time off before starting my search and it helped me go into the process recharged and energized. Additionally, I had the time to read books and learn new things, which I'm almost certain led to better interview performance and even job offers.

### Season makes a difference

If you have the luxury of choice, consider starting your job search in late spring or early summer. My search started in early summer; seeing sunshine and being able to go outside during the day gave a huge boost to my mental health.
